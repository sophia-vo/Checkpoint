What have you done so far?

We met up last week in person to talk about our project. At that meeting, we were able to draw up a quick schema for how we wanted our final project to look, as well as decide on what functionalities we wanted our visualizations to have. So far, our team has designed some of those additional visualizations that will be featured on our final project, including an interactive typing test that compares the user to an individual with Parkinson’s disease. Another one of our visualizations involves a 3D model of a hand (using Three.js), which will give the audience a quick rundown of what Parkinson’s is, how it affects the individual and their typing abilities, and why typing can be an indicator of Parkinson’s. In addition, we have implemented a scrolling functionality for the storytelling aspect, allowing for clean and direct information retention by the audience.

What will be the most challenging of your project to design and why?

There are two parts that are most challenging to design for our project: the 3D hand model and typing test. In the midst of creating the 3D hand model, we discovered some issues that stopped us from creating annotations in certain parts of the hand like we wanted. As a workaround, we downloaded another software that would allow us to do so, but there will be a learning curve to create the hand model and re-configure it back into the website. Another design challenge that we are facing is the typing test. We want the typing test to be a big part of our project, and to make it perfect (or at least up to our standards) will require lots of refactoring and tweaking on our end.

Understanding Parkinson's

Parkinson's disease is a progressive neurodegenerative disorder that primarily affects dopamine-producing ("dopaminergic") neurons in a specific area of the brain called substantia nigra. Symptoms typically develop slowly over years. The progression of symptoms is often different from one person to another due to the diversity of the disease.

Motor Symptoms & The Hand

Parkinson's disease is well-known for its impact on motor control. Symptoms such as tremors, rigidity (stiffness), bradykinesia (slowness of movement), and postural instability can significantly affect daily activities.

The hands are often one of the first areas to show signs. Fine motor skills required for writing, buttoning clothes, or eating can become challenging. The interactive 3D model on the right highlights how different parts of the hand can be affected. Hover over the model to learn more.

Interpreting the Pulse

The "PULSE" button simulates the rhythm of typing, specifically focusing on:

  • Hold Time: The duration a key is pressed.
  • Flight Time: The time between releasing one key and pressing the next.

Variability in these timings can be a subtle indicator of changes in motor control. Common Parkinson's medications like Levodopa, Dopamine Agonists (DA), and MAO-B inhibitors aim to manage these motor symptoms, and their effects can sometimes be observed in fine motor tasks like typing.
